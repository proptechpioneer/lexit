Renters' Rights Act

The Renters' Rights Act passed through English parliament on 27 October 2025 and is anticipated to be introduced in early 2026. The Renters' Rights Act is the most significant reform the private rented sector (PRS) has seen since the Housing Act 1988.

Key Objectives of the Renters' Rights Act
End of Section 21:

No more no-fault evictions—must have a reason to evict.

New eviction process:

Fairer, more transparent dispute resolution.

Long-term tenancy rights:

Greater security for tenants, less flexibility for landlords.

Standardized deposit scheme:

One lifetime deposit instead of multiple security deposits.

Stricter property standards:

Enhanced regulation to improve rental quality.

Revised notice periods:

Longer mandatory notice periods for ending tenancies.

Impact Summary: These changes aim to balance tenant protections with fair processes—landlords will need to adapt to a new regulatory environment.

Frequently Asked Questions

The bill passed through parliament on 27 October 2025. The law will be introduced in three phases.

  • Phase 1 will start on May 1, 2026. This is when the legislation’s major items will become effective, including the abolition of Section 21, the transition to periodic tenancies, rent increases being limited to once per year, and more.

  • Phase 2 will happen between 2026 and 2028. It will include the introduction of the PRS database and the landlord ombudsman.

  • Phase 3 will happen from 2030 onwards, including the application of Awaab's Law and the Decent Homes Standard to the PRS.

It will now be substantially more challenging for landlords to increase the rent. The landlord will only have the right to attempt a rent increase once a year, and must issue a Section 13 Notice with a full two months’ notice to the tenant.


Tenants will also have the right to dispute the rent increase at no cost, forcing the landlord to justify the rent review as fair — adding significant additional hurdles. Furthermore, any rent increase could be delayed for several months or even years before it can take effect.


The entire process will demand more extensive documentation and may lead to prolonged delays, severely impacting cash flow, rental income, and the landlord’s ability to manage property effectively.

Landlords will no longer be able to evict tenants without a specific reason. This means you'll need valid grounds such as rent arrears, property damage, or wanting to sell the property. The process will require more documentation and potentially longer timeframes, affecting cash flow and property management strategies.

Under the RRB all tenancies will now be periodic by default.


There will no longer be a minimum tenancy period, meaning there is no set minimum duration for tenancy agreements. Tenants will be able to give notice at any time, effectively removing the previous minimum tenancy requirements.


Tenants will be able to end their tenancy by giving two months' notice at any time.

Under the Renters' Rights Act, landlords will be required to adopt a far more professional approach to property management, making Buy-to-Let investment no longer suitable for amateur landlords.


Rents will increase less frequently, costs will rise due to increased compliance requirements, and substantial fines will be imposed on landlords who fail to adhere to the new regulations. This shift aims to raise standards but will pose increased challenges and financial risks for all landlords.
